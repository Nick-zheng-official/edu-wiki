# The Legend of Luobohei: After the Academy Days

## Chapter 1: A New Semester Begins

---

The morning sun **dip**ped below the horizon as Luobohei stretched his small black body on the windowsill. Through the window of Xiaoimo's apartment, the city was just waking up—delivery tricycles **ford**ing through narrow alleys, the smell of fresh **bacon** from the **deli** downstairs, and the distant sound of a **bulletin** broadcast from the community center. The whole scene felt like the opening chapter of a story, filled with **propaganda** about peaceful mornings that nobody really believed.

"Luobohei! You're going to be late for school!"

Xiaoimo's voice carried the same **positive** energy as always. The little girl with her signature red hood bounced into the living room, nearly tripping over Luobohei's tail. She was like a **chick** discovering the world for the first time—full of curiosity and warmth.

"Can't I just stay home?" Luobohei tried, though he knew the answer would be the same as every other day.

"No way! You're a student now, and **conservative** or not, school starts at eight." Xiaoimo grabbed her backpack. "Besides, didn't you say you wanted to make friends this semester?"

Luobohei's ears **tilt**ed upward slightly. She had a point. Last semester, he'd spent most of his time sleeping under desks and getting into trouble. But this year... this year would be different.

"Besides," Xiaoimo added with a mischievous smile, "if you don't go, I'll have to tell everyone about that time you knocked over the **aquarium** and blamed it on the wind."

Luobohei shuddered at the memory. The old fish shop owner had nearly **obliged** him to work for a month to pay off the damages. He never wanted a repeat.

---

The walk to school took them through a **provincial** neighborhood that Luobohei had grown to love. It wasn't much—just old houses with moss growing on the rooftops, a small **aquarium** shop run by an old man who always gave him free fish treats, and a tiny park where the grass **bloom**ed in spring.

The old man at the aquarium was a **caretaker** of sorts—a spirit who watched over the small creatures in his care. He had once told Luobohei about the old days, when the spirit world and human world existed in **accordance** with each other, before the **drought** of trust between them. The old man was like a **craftsman** of peace, carefully maintaining balance between the two worlds.

The stories spoke of a time when spirits and humans lived together without **racial** tension or conflict. It was during what historians called the "**Arctic** Peace"—a period when the world was blanketed in understanding rather than snow.

Xiaoimo **trim**med a stray lock of hair from her face as they walked. "By the way, did you hear? There's supposed to be a transfer student joining our class today."

"Human or..." Luobohei left the question hanging.

"The rumor is human." Xiaoimo shrugged. "But you know how rumors **dissolve** around here. Remember when everyone said the new teacher was a spirit?"

"Turned out to be true, though."

"Fair point."

As they approached the school gate, Luobohei **detach**ed from Xiaoimo's shoulder and hopped down to explore on his own. That's when he noticed it—a single black feather, pristine and gleaming, lying near the entrance. It looked exactly like one of his own. A **brass** clasp held it together, unusual for a wild bird's feather.

He sniffed it cautiously. Nothing unusual, but something felt... off.

"Luobohei! Stop **beggar**ing around and get to class!" Xiaoimo's voice **amplify**ed down the hallway.

With one last look at the feather, Luobohei tucked it into his fur and ran after her.

---

Classroom 3-B was already buzzing when they arrived. The **aerial** view from the second floor didn't reveal much, but from ground level, Luobohei could see the familiar chaos—desks arranged in uneven rows, chalk dust floating in the sunlight, and his classmates already divided into their usual clusters.

Luobohei made a beeline for his seat by the window, the same spot where he'd slept through most of last semester's math class. As he settled in, he felt the black feather in his fur grow slightly warmer, almost like a small **blaze** of energy.

The classroom door slid open, and the teacher entered followed by a girl he didn't recognize.

"Class, this is our new transfer student. Please welcome her warmly."

The girl stood at the front of the room, her dark hair falling past her shoulders. She scanned the room with eyes that seemed to **facilitate** a strange sensation in Luobohei's chest—recognition, perhaps? Or something else?

"Name's Lin Yue," she said simply. Her gaze landed on Luobohei for just a moment, and the cat spirit felt a chill run down his spine.

_She can see me_, he thought. _Definitely a spirit._

---

The morning classes **grind** on as they always do. Luobohei found himself watching Lin Yue more than paying attention to the lesson. She sat two rows ahead, taking notes with **industrious** dedication, her posture perfect. Everything about her seemed... controlled.

At lunch, Xiaoimo dragged Luobohei to the cafeteria, where the smell of **charcoal**-grilled skewers filled the air. They found a spot by the window, and Luobohei curled up on the bench while Xiaoimo ate her meal. On the table sat a small **bouquet** of plastic flowers—leftover from someone's birthday celebration.

"You should make friends with Lin Yue," Xiaoimo said between bites. "She seems nice."

"She's not human," Luobohei replied quietly.

Xiaoimo's chopsticks paused mid-air. "Are you sure?"

"One hundred percent." He touched the feather hidden in his fur. "And I think she knows what I am."

"You're overthinking it," Xiaoimo said, returning to her food. "Just be yourself. Maybe she likes cats."

---

After lunch, as the class returned to Room 3-B, Lin Yue approached Luobohei's desk. The other students filed out for their afternoon **errand**—cleaning duty in the courtyard.

"Luobohei, right?" Her voice was soft but carried an edge of **intensive** focus. "The spirit cat from the forest. I've read about your ability to shift through shadows, creating portals and manipulating space. Impressive, for someone so young. Your kind can sense energy through the **abdomen**, right?"

Luobohei looked up at her, his yellow eyes narrowing. "And you're from...?"

"The Hall of Mist. We're part of the **fleet** of spirit organizations that maintain order between the human and spirit realms." She sat down across from him, her movements precise and calculated. "I've been sent to observe you."

"Observe me? Why?"

Lin Yue's expression remained neutral, but something flickered in her eyes. "Let's just say... certain parties have taken interest in your recent activities. The movie incident. The spirit world **outbreak** last spring. They're wondering if you're a **solution** to future problems... or a problem itself."

Luobohei felt his fur bristle. The way she spoke reminded him of the old **democracy** meetings among spirits—where every voice mattered, but some mattered more than others. "I'm just a cat. I don't cause trouble."

"Trouble has a way of finding you." Lin Yue stood up. "But don't worry. I'm not here to cause problems for you. In fact..." She glanced toward the window, where the old oak tree cast shadows across the courtyard. "I might be the one who can help you."

"Help me with what?"

"The sealed forest behind the school." Lin Yue's voice dropped to barely a whisper. "Something is waking up there. Something old. And if what I've heard is true... you might be the only one who can stop it. Something **evil** that was sealed away long ago."

Before Luobohei could respond, the afternoon bell rang. Lin Yue returned to her seat as if nothing had happened, leaving the cat spirit with more questions than answers.

---

The rest of the day passed in a **fragment**ed blur of classes and half-hearted attention. When the final bell rang, Xiaoimo found Luobohei staring out the window at the dense trees beyond the athletic field.

"The forest?" she asked, following his gaze. "The school says it's off-limits. Something about dangerous wildlife."

"It's not wildlife." Luobohei's voice was quiet. "It's older than that. Some say it's where the spirits **reproduce** their ancient powers. A place where even the **pirate** spirits of old came to hide their treasures."

Xiaoimo sat down beside him. "Want to talk about it?"

"Not yet." He **detach**ed the black feather from his fur and held it up to the fading light. It shimmered with an iridescent quality that seemed almost alive. The feather seemed to **brim** with some unseen energy as he held it closer. "I need to understand this first."

"But Luobohei..." Xiaoimo's voice was gentle, "even if you can't stop whatever's coming, remember—you don't have to carry everything alone. You have me. You have Tu Mo. You're not in **detention** for being different. You belong here, with us."

Luobohei looked at her. In that moment, he saw the same **insight** in her eyes that the old caretaker had—a deep understanding that went beyond her years.

---

That evening, as Xiaoimo did her homework at the kitchen table, Luobohei sat by the window watching the city lights **bloom** across the skyline. His phone buzzed—a message from Tu Mo, the self-proclaimed "Captain" of the Hygiene team.

> "Heard there's a new spirit in town. Any trouble?"
> "Not yet."
> "Good. And about that forest—keep your nose out of it. Some things are better left alone."

Luobohei set the phone down. So even Tu Mo knew something. That didn't make him feel any better.

"Bad news?" Xiaoimo asked without looking up from her math problems.

"Just the usual." Luobohei curled up on the windowsill. "Paranoia. Secrets. The weight of being a **crutch** for everyone's peace of mind."

"You say that like you're not just a cat who likes fish."

Luobohei couldn't help but laugh. When she put it that way, everything did seem simpler.

"Xiaoimo?"

"Yeah?"

"Thanks for... you know. Being my **shelter** and all."

Xiaoimo smiled, finally looking up from her homework. "Anytime, Luobohei. Anytime."

---

As night fell over the city, Luobohei made his way to the roof of the apartment building. From there, he could see the distant silhouette of the sealed forest, its trees standing like silent sentinels against the starry sky. The forest was like a **crib** for ancient powers—holding something precious, something that needed protection.

He thought about the feather in his fur—warm, pulsing with faint energy. He thought about Lin Yue, with her **aerial** grace and guarded secrets. He thought about Tu Mo's warning and the old stories about spirits who once **dwell**ed in those woods.

The world was changing. He could feel it in his whiskers, in the way the wind carried whispers on the breeze.

But for now, he was just Luobohei. A cat who happened to be a spirit. A student who happened to be caught up in something much bigger than himself.

And tomorrow, he would go back to school.

He would learn.

He would watch.

He would wait.

Because some mysteries weren't meant to be solved in a single day. They were **indefinite**—like the journey of a thousand miles, they began with a single step.

Tonight, that step was rest.

Tomorrow, the real adventure would begin.

---

**End of Chapter 1**

_The forest watches. The feather glows. And somewhere in the shadows, a pair of eyes—far older and more powerful than any cat could imagine—continues to observe. But that's a story for another chapter._

## Chapter 2: The Weight of Feathers

---

The next morning arrived with a **grim** surprise.

Luobohei woke to find his black fur **filthy** with dust and what appeared to be... ash? He **scratch**ed his ear, trying to remember. The rooftop. He must have wandered onto the building's **electrical** system overnight—some sort of transformer that gave off a faint **puff** of smoke when overloaded.

Wait. Ash?

He jumped down from the windowsill and padded to the window. The view of the city was obscured by a strange haze, and in the distance—the sealed forest was **stationary**, but something about it felt wrong. The usual **horizon** line was blurred by what looked like **dew** mixed with smoke.

This wasn't natural.

Xiaoimo burst into the room with her usual morning energy, nearly tripping over Luobohei. She took one look at him and burst out laughing.

"You look like you fought a **mess** of **spinach** and lost!" She giggled.

"Thanks for the **remark**," Luobohei said dryly. "Very **excellent** observation skills."

"Bad night?" she asked, suddenly concerned.

"I don't know yet." He shook his fur, sending **filthy** dust **puff**ing into the air. "But I think something is changing."

---

At school, the atmosphere was **diverse** from usual. Students clustered in groups, whispering behind their hands. Luobohei caught fragments of conversation as he followed Xiaoimo to their classroom.

"—the forest was glowing last night—"
"—my dad said it's just **electrical** problems—"
"—no way, I saw it myself, the trees were **startle**ing—"

Lin Yue was already at her desk when they entered. She looked up as Luobohei passed, and for a moment, something **grim** flickered across her face. Then it was gone, replaced by her usual neutral expression.

After the morning bell, the teacher announced a **parking** lot cleanup duty for the afternoon—the school was expecting visitors for some event. "And please," the teacher added, "stay away from the back fields. The forest area is still off-limits."

The teacher tried to sound casual, but Luobohei noticed how her voice **expend**ed extra effort on the last part.

---

During lunch, Luobohei found himself sitting alone on the school roof—a favorite **recreation** spot of his. He was trying to make sense of his strange dreams when the door opened behind him.

Lin Yue climbed through, her movements graceful despite the awkward space.

"Mind if I join you?" she asked, though she was already sitting down.

Luobohei didn't **intend** to be rude, but curiosity won over caution. "What's happening with the forest?"

Lin Yue was quiet for a long moment, her eyes scanning the distant treeline. "The seal is weakening. I **nominate** this as the reason you've been having those dreams."

"Dreams?" He tried to sound **dubious**, but his voice came out more curious.

"Don't play games with me." Her tone was **intensive**, focused. "You're not the only one who came here with purpose. And if you're going to help, you need to **acknowledge** what's at stake."

She pulled out a small, **rustic** wooden box from her bag. It was old, with symbols carved into its surface that Luobohei recognized from his forest spirit teachings.

"From the **biography** of our ancestors," Lin Yue said quietly, opening the box to reveal a small **gloss**y stone inside. "The Monarch Stone. It was used to create the seal hundreds of years ago."

Luobohei's fur stood on end. "Why are you showing me this?"

"Because it's **restless** energy is increasing. The stone is responding to something in the forest." She closed the box carefully. "The seal was created to **obstruct** something—a being so powerful that even the Hall of Mist couldn't destroy it. They could only trap it."

"What happens when the seal breaks?"

Lin Yue's eyes were **horizon**-less, dark. "Then we find out if this world has enough **stability** to survive."

---

The afternoon was spent in a strange limbo. Luobohei tried to focus on his **parking** lot cleanup duty, but his mind kept **dwell**ing on Lin Yue's words. The cleanup was **fruitless** in more ways than one—he kept making mistakes, knocking over cones, and missing trash that others easily spotted.

"You're being **lame** today," Xiaoimo said, not unkindly. She was helping him collect stray bottles and papers. "What's wrong?"

"Bad night. Strange dreams."

"Something about the forest?" She glanced toward the forbidden area. "I heard someone say the boundary ropes are glowing."

"Glowing?"

"Like there's something inside pushing against them." She tried to sound casual, but Luobohei could hear the **hindrance** in her voice—the worry she was trying to hide.

That evening, as they walked home, they passed an old woman collecting bottles from the **parking** lot. She had a **suspender** belt full of them, clinking with each step.

"Excuse me," Luobohei heard himself say—the old woman was always nice to him, giving him treats when Xiaoimo wasn't looking. "Did you see anything strange last night?"

The old woman looked at him with eyes that seemed to see more than they should. "The forest remembers what the world forgets, little one." She smiled, toothless. "Your grandmother would have known. Mine told me stories."

Xiaoimo's hand found Luobohei's paw. "Let's go."

---

That night, Luobohei had the dream again.

He was in the forest, but it wasn't the sealed one. This was his forest—the one from before. Trees that **bloom**ed with light, spirits that **dwell**ed in harmony with the world. A place where the **dew** settled on leaves like diamonds.

And in the center, something old. A figure that seemed to be made of shadows and starlight.

The being turned. It had eyes like the night sky—**diverse** galaxies swirling in an endless void.

_"You came back,"_ it said. Its voice was like wind through leaves. _"But you're not ready yet. The path is **obstruct**ed by those who **spite** what they don't understand."_

"I don't understand," Luobohei said.

_"No. But you will."_ The figure began to fade. _"When the seal breaks, come to me. The **monarch** of this forest has been sleeping long enough. Wake me."_

Luobohei woke with a **startle**, heart pounding.

The feather in his fur was warm. Almost hot.

---

The next day was Saturday—no school. Luobohei woke to find Xiaoimo's grandmother visiting, sitting at the kitchen table with a cup of tea. The old woman had a **grim** look on her face.

"Grandma," Xiaoimo was saying, "you can't just—"

"I know what I saw." The grandmother's voice was firm. "The forest was **luminous** last night. Not electrical—luminous. Like spirit light."

Luobohei felt his fur **bristle**. The grandmother could see spirits? That was... he didn't know that.

"Luobohei." The grandmother's voice called him over. "Come here, little one."

He approached cautiously. The grandmother's eyes—Xiaoimo's eyes, really, but older, weathered—studied him with an **overwhelming** intensity.

"You're from the forest, aren't you?" she asked. Not accusing. Just knowing.

Luobohei didn't know what to say. His years of hiding seemed suddenly **fruitless**.

"Don't worry." The grandmother smiled—the same warm smile Xiaoimo had. "In my day, we knew many spirits. Before the **embargo** on spirit knowledge, before the walls went up." She reached out and **lubricate**d his fur gently with her weathered hand. "You carry a heavy burden, little one. But you don't carry it alone."

"What do you know about the seal?" Luobohei asked quietly.

"Enough to know it was never meant to last forever." Her voice was **grim**. "The being inside—it was never truly evil. It was just... different. And difference breeds fear. Fear breeds **spite**. Spite breeds seals."

"But Lin Yue said—"

"Lin Yue is a child of the Hall of Mist. Their **narrative** is not the only one." The grandmother stood, joints creaking like old floorboards. "The being in the forest—it's been there since before spirits and humans divided. It protected both. Until they turned on it."

Xiaoimo had been listening quietly. Now she spoke up. "What do we do?"

The grandmother looked at her granddaughter with an expression that broke Luobohei's heart—a mixture of love and **grim** inevitability.

"We prepare. We **acknowledge** what's coming. And we hope—" She paused. "We hope it's not too late for hope."

---

That afternoon, Luobohei found himself at the edge of the prohibited zone. The boundary ropes were indeed glowing—a soft, pulsing light that seemed almost alive.

He could feel the energy pressing against the seal. The **faculty** of the forest spirit realm, calling to him.

The feather in his fur was almost unbearable now.

"Thought I'd find you here."

Luobohei turned to see Lin Yue approaching. She looked tired—the **excellent** composure from before was showing cracks.

"What are you going to do?" she asked.

"I don't know. The dream—"

"I know about the dream." She sat beside him, not too close. "The Hall of Mist has been monitoring the seal. When it started weakening, we thought it was just the natural cycle. But now..."

"Now?"

"Now we're not sure if the being inside is waking up... or if something else is pushing from outside."

Luobohei felt a chill. "What do you mean?"

Lin Yue was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was barely above a whisper. "The seal was made by the old spirits—monarchs of their realms. But recently, we've detected energy signatures that don't match any known spirit **faculty**."

"You think someone is trying to break it?"

"I think someone wants what's inside." Lin Yue's eyes were dark. "And if they succeed, we won't just lose the forest. We'll lose everything."

Luobohei looked at the glowing boundary. The **mess** of energy signatures was almost visible to him now—strange, **dubious** patterns that seemed wrong.

"How long do we have?"

"Weeks. Maybe days." Lin Yue stood. "The Hall of Mist is sending a team. But until then..."

"Until then?"

"Until then, we watch. We protect. We prepare." She looked at him—this spirit cat who had fallen into a world of politics and ancient seals and impossible choices. "You didn't ask for this. But you're the only one who can do what needs to be done."

Luobohei didn't know what to say.

"I know you're scared," Lin Yue added, her voice softening. "The world is **restless** with change. But fear isn't weakness. It's wisdom."

She walked away, leaving him alone with the glowing forest and the weight of impossible choices.

The feather pulsed in his fur.

And somewhere deep in the sealed forest, something ancient stirred in its sleep.

---

**End of Chapter 2**

_The seal weakens. The dream calls. And in the shadows of old promises, the shape of things to come begins to form. But some truths are better left buried—for now._

## Chapter 3: The Old Man in the Shadows

---

The following Monday dawned with an **extravagant** display of autumn colors. The trees lining the school **lane** were ablaze with red and gold, their leaves dancing in the crisp morning breeze. But Luobohei barely noticed. His mind was too occupied with the **revelation** from his dream—the Forest Monarch's words echoing in his head: _"You came back."_

What did it mean?

Xiaoimo noticed his distraction immediately. "You've been staring into space all morning," she said as they walked to school. "Is it the feather again?"

Luobohei touched the black feather tucked in his fur. It was warm, almost alive. "It's not just the feather. It's... everything. The forest. The dreams. Lin Yue. I feel like I'm missing something important."

"Maybe you should **contact** Tu Mo," Xiaoimo suggested. "He knows more about spirits than anyone I know."

Before Luobohei could respond, they rounded the corner and saw it—a **banner** strung across the school entrance. It read: **"Annual School Spirit Festival - This Saturday!"**

Spirit Festival? That was unusual. Schools didn't usually celebrate spirit-related events—not since the **embargo** on spirit knowledge.

As they approached, Luobohei noticed something else. A figure leaning against the gatepost, watching them with an amused expression. He was an older man with silver hair tied back in a messy bun, wearing a worn leather jacket and smoking a pipe. There was something about him that made Luobohei's fur **bristle**—a sense of power, old and deep.

"Xiaoimo," the man said, his voice gravelly but warm. "You've grown since last I saw you."

"Old Ge!" Xiaoimo exclaimed, running over to hug him. "What are you doing here?"

Old Ge. The name clicked. This was the mysterious figure from the foreshadowing—the one who'd been watching Luobohei.

The old man's eyes shifted to Luobohei. "And this must be the famous cat spirit everyone's talking about." He took a puff of his pipe, smoke curling around his face. "You're smaller than I expected."

Luobohei narrowed his eyes. "Who are you?"

"Just an **old chap** who knows a thing or two about spirits," Old Ge said with a grin. "But enough about me. Let's talk about you." He nodded toward the feather in Luobohei's fur. "That's no ordinary feather. Where'd you get it?"

"It was left at the school gate," Luobohei said cautiously.

Old Ge's smile faded. "I see." He looked toward the sealed forest, his expression **grim**. "The seal is weakening. You felt it, didn't you?"

Before Luobohei could answer, the school bell rang. Xiaoimo grabbed his paw. "We have to go! Old Ge, wait for us after school!"

Old Ge gave a lazy salute. "Don't worry, kid. I'll be here."

---

The morning passed in a blur. Luobohei couldn't focus on his lessons—his mind kept returning to Old Ge. There was something **fascinating** about him, like an ancient book waiting to be opened. During recess, Lin Yue approached him.

"Old Ge is here," she said without preamble. "You should be careful around him."

"Why? Who is he?"

"One of the oldest spirits alive," Lin Yue said quietly. "He was there when the seal was created. Some say he helped build it." She paused. "He's also... **inconsistent**. One day he's helping, the next he's gone. The Hall of Mist doesn't trust him."

Luobohei thought about the old man's eyes—wise, amused, but with something deeper lurking beneath. "Why is he here?"

"To see you," Lin Yue said. "I'd **reckon** he's been waiting for this moment for a long time."

---

After school, Old Ge was waiting under the old oak tree by the **parking** lot. He'd set up a small folding chair and was smoking his pipe, watching the students file out.

"Come," he said, patting the ground beside him. "Let's talk."

Luobohei approached cautiously, while Xiaoimo hovered nearby.

"First things first," Old Ge said, blowing smoke rings. "That feather. It's not just a feather. It's a **key**—a fragment of the Forest Monarch's own essence. When the seal was created, the Monarch gave parts of itself to the guardians. Your ancestor was one of those guardians."

Luobohei froze. "My... ancestor?"

"Your mother," Old Ge said simply. "She was the last guardian of the Eastern Forest. When the seal began to weaken, she sent you here—to the human world—to keep you safe."

"But why me?"

"Because you're special," Old Ge said. "You're not just any forest spirit. You're a **binary** being—part forest spirit, part... something else. Something older."

"What do you mean?"

Old Ge leaned back, looking up at the sky. "Long ago, before the **embargo**, before the walls between worlds went up, there was a time when spirits and humans lived in harmony. The Forest Monarch was the guardian of that harmony. But then... fear took over. Humans feared what they didn't understand. Spirits feared losing their way of life. The **brigade** of spirit organizations—like the Hall of Mist—they decided the safest thing was to lock the Monarch away. To seal it where no one could reach it."

"But Xiaoimo's grandmother said the Monarch protected both sides," Luobohei said.

Old Ge smiled sadly. "She's right. The Monarch didn't choose sides. It chose balance. And balance is a dangerous thing to those who crave control."

He pulled a small, **oblong** box from his pocket and opened it. Inside was a piece of crystal, glowing faintly with the same light as Luobohei's feather.

"This is the other half of your key," Old Ge said. "Your mother gave it to me for safekeeping. She knew one day you'd need it."

Luobohei stared at the crystal. "What do I do with it?"

"You **undertake** the journey no one else can," Old Ge said. "You go into the forest. You find the Monarch. And you wake it up before whoever is trying to break the seal gets there first."

"But Lin Yue said the Monarch is dangerous!"

"Lin Yue was raised on the Hall of Mist's **narrative**," Old Ge said dismissively. "They tell stories to justify their choices. The Monarch isn't dangerous. It's... lonely. And it's the only thing that can restore the balance."

---

That evening, Luobohei sat on the apartment rooftop, holding both the feather and the crystal. They hummed in harmony, a low vibration that seemed to resonate in his bones.

Xiaoimo joined him, bringing a blanket. "What did Old Ge say?"

"He said I'm supposed to wake the Forest Monarch," Luobohei said quietly. "That it's the only way to stop whatever's breaking the seal."

Xiaoimo wrapped the blanket around them. "Do you believe him?"

"I don't know," Luobohei admitted. "But I know one thing—I can't just sit here and do nothing. Not when the forest is calling to me."

He looked at the sealed forest in the distance, now glowing faintly even in the twilight.

"Whatever happens," Xiaoimo said firmly, "I'm with you. We'll figure this out together."

Luobohei smiled. Even in the face of ancient secrets and impossible choices, he wasn't alone.

---

The next day, Old Ge was gone. He'd left a note for Luobohei, written on a scrap of paper:

> "The **optimal** time to enter the forest is at dawn, when the veil between worlds is thinnest. Take the crystal. Trust your instincts. And remember—some doors are meant to be opened, even if you're scared of what's on the other side."

Luobohei folded the note and tucked it into his fur. He didn't know what the future held, but he knew one thing: the time for waiting was over.

---

That night, Luobohei had the dream again. But this time, it was different.

The Forest Monarch stood before him, its starry eyes soft. _"You have the key,"_ it said. _"But you must understand—waking me will change everything. The **stability** you know will be gone."_

"I don't care about stability if it means losing what matters," Luobohei said.

The Monarch smiled. _"Then come. The forest awaits its guardian."_

Luobohei woke with a start, the crystal warm in his paw.

Dawn was breaking.

## Chapter 4: Into the Whispering Woods

---

Dawn broke like **magic** over the city. The first light painted the sky in soft hues of pink and gold, and the air was crisp with the promise of autumn. Luobohei stood at the edge of the sealed forest, the feather and crystal key glowing in his paws. Behind him, Xiaoimo waited, her eyes wide with a mix of fear and determination.

"Are you sure about this?" she asked for the tenth time.
